How to Track Your Pull-Up Progress (and Why It Works)

July 11, 2026 · 6 min read

Ask someone how their pull-up training is going and you'll usually get a feeling: “pretty good, I think?” Ask someone who logs their sets and you'll get an answer: “38 reps last week, best set of 9, third week in a row above my goal.”

The difference between those two answers is the difference between guessing and knowing — and it's usually the difference between plateauing and progressing. Here's how to track pull-ups in a way that takes seconds and actually changes your results.

Why tracking works

Three reasons, all boringly practical:

  • Progress requires overload, and overload requires a record. You get stronger by doing slightly more than last time — one more rep, one more set, a little added weight. But “more than last time” is only meaningful if you know what last time was. Memory rounds everything off; a log doesn't.
  • Visible progress is motivating. Strength gains are slow enough that day to day you feel nothing. A chart of the last two months shows you what a single workout can't: the line goes up. On the days you don't feel like training, that line is often the thing that gets you to the bar.
  • A log keeps you honest. Skipped weeks disappear from memory but not from a calendar. A quick glance at a heatmap of your training days tells you whether you've actually been consistent or just feel like you have.

What to record

Keep it minimal — the best tracking system is the one you'll still be using in six months. For each set, you want:

  • Date — when you did it
  • Reps — how many
  • Type — regular bodyweight, weighted, or assisted
  • Weight — added weight for weighted sets, or assistance used for assisted sets
  • Notes (optional) — anything worth remembering: new grip, felt easy, elbow niggle

That's it. Resist the urge to track ten more variables — tempo, rest times, heart rate. More fields mean more friction, and friction is what kills tracking habits. You can always add detail in a note when something is genuinely worth noting.

The numbers that actually matter

Once you're logging, four metrics tell you nearly everything:

  • Weekly volume — total reps per week. This is your workload dial: the number to nudge upward slowly over months and the first thing to check when progress stalls (too little) or your arms feel wrecked (too much, too fast).
  • Best single set — your max reps in one unbroken set. The classic strength benchmark. It moves slowly, so test it only every few weeks; the rest of the time, let it climb on its own in the background.
  • Training days — how many days a week you actually get to the bar. Consistency predicts long-term results better than any single workout. Streaks are the fun version of this metric.
  • The trend — this month versus last month. Individual workouts bounce around with sleep and stress; the monthly trend smooths out the noise and shows whether your training is actually working.

Notebook, spreadsheet, or app?

All three work. Honestly.

A notebook is free and simple, but it can't add anything up — your weekly volume and trends stay invisible unless you compute them yourself, and the history lives in one physical place you can lose.

A spreadsheet can compute anything, and if you enjoy building formulas it's a fine choice. In practice, most spreadsheets die at the entry step: opening a sheet and finding the right cell on your phone, mid-workout, with sweaty hands, is exactly enough friction to skip “just this once,” and then forever.

A dedicated app earns its place by making the entry step nearly free — a couple of taps between sets — and computing the stats, records, and streaks automatically. Turning the log into progress

A log is only useful if it changes what you do next. Simple ways to use it:

  • Beat last week by a little. Add one rep somewhere — to any set, or as an extra mini-set at the end. One rep a week is over fifty a year.
  • Add sets before reps. If your sets of 6 won't become sets of 7, do four sets of 6 instead of three. Volume drives the max up indirectly.
  • Graduate to weighted work. Once you're comfortably into double digits, small added weights restart the fast-progress phase. Log the weight and watch a second set of records start climbing.
  • Set a modest weekly goal. A target you can hit on a busy week — say 30 or 50 reps — beats an ambitious one you'll abandon in February. Raise it when it stops feeling like a stretch.

Mistakes to avoid

  • Testing your max every session. Max attempts are fatiguing and informationally cheap — the number barely moves week to week. Build with sub-maximal sets; test occasionally.
  • Chasing the streak into the ground. Consistency metrics should encourage training, not punish recovery. Rest days are part of the program — pick a tracker or a rule that tolerates them. (Pull-Up Tracker's streaks have a built-in one-day grace period for exactly this reason.)
  • Letting one bad week end the habit. A gap in the log is data, not failure. The log's job is to make restarting easy: you can see exactly where you left off and pick it back up.
  • Counting sloppy reps. If the reps get shorter as the numbers get bigger, the progress is fictional. Decide what a rep is — full hang to chin over bar is the usual standard — and count only those.

Start today, not Monday

Tracking has a compounding quality: the longer the history, the more motivating it gets. The chart of your first week is a few lonely bars; the chart of your first year is genuinely satisfying to look at. Do a set today — whatever you can — write it down, and you've started the baseline that every future personal record will be measured against.
